FAQs

Hearing loss is diagnosed through comprehensive audiometric testing, which includes pure-tone audiometry, speech testing, and sometimes middle ear analysis. These tests help determine the type and exact degree of your hearing loss.

While some types of conductive hearing loss (like blockages or infections) can be medically reversed, most sensorineural hearing loss (damage to the inner ear) is permanent. However, it can be effectively managed and improved with advanced hearing aids or implants.

Yes, prolonged exposure to loud noises or a single sudden extremely loud sound can cause noise-induced hearing loss. This type of hearing loss is permanent but entirely preventable by using adequate hearing protection in noisy environments.

Yes, age-related hearing loss (presbycusis) is very common. As we age, the tiny hair cells in the inner ear naturally begin to break down, leading to gradual hearing loss, particularly with high-frequency sounds.

Treatments depend heavily on the underlying cause. They range from simple medical procedures for ear wax blockages to sophisticated digital hearing aids, assistive listening devices, and sometimes surgical options like cochlear implants.

Absolutely. Hearing loss can occur in children due to genetics, infections during pregnancy, complications at birth, or frequent ear infections. Early detection is crucial to prevent delays in their speech and cognitive development.

Yes, genetics play a significant role in many types of hearing loss. If you have a strong family history of hearing difficulties, you may be at a higher risk and should schedule regular audiological evaluations.

Yes, some lifestyle changes that can help prevent hearing loss include protecting your ears from loud noises, avoiding unnecessary exposure to ototoxic drugs, and maintaining good overall health.
